# Fai in Silver Age: The Math Behind the Fire
 
Fai (Young Hero) is one of the most mechanically satisfying heroes in Silver Age. He rewards players who understand his engine, punishes those who don't respect his chain-building requirement, and has a surprisingly deep EV (expressed value) ceiling once the pieces come together. This guide breaks down exactly how Fai works, what the math looks like turn by turn, and how to sideboard correctly for the format's key matchups.
 
---
 
## Who Is Fai?
 
Fai is a **Young Draconic Ninja** hero — the Silver Age version of the hero, legal specifically because Silver Age requires the Young subtype. Silver Age features young heroes exclusively, and adult hero cards are not legal in the format. This distinction matters: Fai's young hero card is the version you register in your 55-card pool, and its ability is identical in structure to what drives the entire deck.
 
His hero ability, confirmed via official card text, reads:
 
> You may start the game with a Phoenix Flame in your graveyard.
> **Once per Turn Instant** — [3 Resource]: Return a Phoenix Flame from your graveyard to your hand. This ability costs [1 Resource] less for each Draconic chain link you control.
 
At zero Draconic chain links, this costs 3 resources — prohibitively expensive. At 3 Draconic chain links, it costs **nothing**. The entire deck is built to hit that magic number of 3, and ideally 4, every single turn.
 
---
 
## The Two Numbers That Define Fai: 4 and 7
 
Before understanding any individual card, you need to internalise two numbers.
 
### The Number 4
 
**Chain link 4 is Fai's power ceiling.** Two things happen at chain link 4:
 
1. **Rupture** triggers — Lava Burst deals 2 arcane damage on top of its physical power, and Salt the Wound scales off all damage dealt prior in the chain.
2. **Compounding Anger becomes free** — it costs 3 minus 1 per Draconic link, so at 3 links it costs 0. A free 5-power attack at chain link 3 into a Rupture finisher at link 4 is Fai's fundamental win condition.
 
Every card in the deck exists either to **build toward link 4** (starters and extenders) or to **fire at link 4** (finishers). Understanding which role a card plays is the foundation of good Fai sequencing.
 
### The Number 7
 
**7 is the expressed value benchmark per card on peak turns.** According to Hamilton's expressed value theory, a top-tier constructed deck should generate 3.5+ EV per card on average. Fai's best individual card combinations push well beyond this:
 
- Rise from the Ashes (0-cost, go again) pumps the next Draconic attack by +3
- Fire that Burns Within (0-cost, go again) at base is 2 power — but discarding a Phoenix Flame pushes it to 4 power
- **Combine both:** Rise from the Ashes (+3) on Fire that Burns Within (2 base + 2 from Phoenix Flame discard) = **7 power** on a single attack from two 0-cost cards
 
7 power forces your opponent to use at minimum two block cards to fully absorb the hit — or take significant damage through. This is what separates Fai's peak turns from his average ones.

## The Engine: Phoenix Flame and the Chain Loop
 
The Phoenix Flame is the heart of the deck. On its own it's a 0-power attack. But it enables three separate return mechanisms:
 
| Return method | Condition | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Fai hero ability | 3 Draconic chain links | 0 resources |
| Rise from the Ashes | Optional — returns from graveyard | 0 (part of the card effect) |
| Flamecall Awakening | On attack, if another red was played | 0 (search from deck) |
 
With all three mechanisms in the deck, you are essentially **guaranteed** Phoenix Flame access every turn once the chain is built. This means the Phoenix Flame discard into Fire that Burns Within is never a true loss — you will always get it back within the same turn or the following one.
 
> **Key insight:** The Phoenix Flame discard is intentional. Fire that Burns Within at 2 power hits below the single-block breakpoint — one blue card stops it completely. At 4 power (after the discard), your opponent needs two cards to fully block it or takes damage through. That 1-card difference in blocking requirement is worth more than the Phoenix Flame in most situations.

## Starters: Building the Chain
 
Fai's starters are the 0-cost Draconic attacks that initiate every turn. The article framework from *The Rathe Times* describes these as "attacks that automatically have go again" — and they are the most resource-efficient cards in the deck.
 
### The 0-Cost Core (×6 total)
 
All three of these share the same stat line: **0 cost, 3 power, 2 defense, go again.**
 
**Brand with Cinderclaw (red)** makes your *next* attack this combat chain Draconic. This is critical — it lets non-Draconic cards contribute to your chain link count, enabling the hero ability and Searing Emberblade discounts even on turns where you draw non-Draconic cards.
 
**Rising Resentment (red)** on-hit reduces the cost of your next card by 1. Against heroes who cannot block everything, this effectively funds Flamecall Awakening or Art of the Dragon for free off the hit — turning a 3-EV starter into a 4-EV play.
 
**Ronin Renegade (red)** is vanilla — no effect, pure Draconic chain link. Its value is entirely in the link it adds. It is the legitimate first cut against 5-card-hand heroes like Lyath Goldmane who can absorb 3-power attacks cleanly with a single card.
 
### The 1-Cost Starters
 
**Flamecall Awakening** is the most important upgrade in the current deck. At 1 cost, 3 power, go again — it searches your deck for a Phoenix Flame on attack if another red was played this turn. Since your chain always starts with a 0-cost red starter, this condition is almost always met. With Flamecall Awakening active, you have **four separate Phoenix Flame access mechanisms** in the deck, making your engine essentially redundancy-proof.
 
**Mounting Anger** is deceptively complex. Its on-hit effect banishes an attack card from your hand (with cost less than your Draconic link count), gives it +1 power, and lets you play it immediately. The ceiling: at 3 Draconic links, banish Compounding Anger (cost normally 3, now 0 from link reduction), replay it for **6 power** on a 1-cost starter's on-hit. This is one of Fai's highest single-card EV ceilings in the C/R category.

## Extenders: Pushing the Turn Further
 
Extenders are the cards that take a 3-link chain and push it to 4 or 5. They are what separates Fai's 12 EV average turns from his 17+ EV peak turns.
 
### Rise from the Ashes (×2) — Never Cut
 
```
Cost: 0 | Effect: Go again. Next Draconic or Ninja attack +3 power.
Optionally return a Phoenix Flame from graveyard to hand.
```
 
This card does three things simultaneously: extends the chain (go again), pumps the following attack (+3), and recovers the engine (Phoenix Flame return). At 0 cost, it is the most efficient extender in the deck. The +3 pump turns:
 
- 0-cost starters (3 power) → **6 power** (requires 2 blocks)
- Fire that Burns Within with Phoenix Flame discard (4 power) → **7 power** (Hamilton's benchmark)
- Compounding Anger (5 power) → **8 power** at 0 cost
 
### Rake Over the Coals (×2) — Never Cut
 
```
Cost: 0 | Type: Draconic Instant | Effect: All Draconic attacks get +1 power this turn.
```
 
This is an **instant** — not an attack, not a chain link. It can be played during the combat chain reaction step, meaning you can wait to see your opponent's block decisions before playing it to push damage over their threshold. In a 4-Draconic-attack turn, Rake Over the Coals adds **+4 EV at 0 cost**. Every 3-power starter becomes 4 power, every 5-power Compounding Anger becomes 6. Stacked with Art of the Dragon: Fire, every Draconic attack gains +2 power — turning your entire chain above the single-block breakpoint for zero resources.
 
### Art of the Dragon: Fire (×2) — Never Cut
 
```
Cost: 1 | Effect: Your Draconic attacks get +1 power until end of turn. Go again.
```
 
Unlike Rake (an instant with no chain link), Art of the Dragon is an attack action that **adds a Draconic chain link** while buffing every subsequent attack. The extra chain link brings you closer to free Searing Emberblade and free hero ability. Stacked with Rake Over the Coals: **+2 to all Draconic attacks** for the cost of 1 resource and 1 card.
 
### Enflame the Firebrand (×2) — Conditional
 
```
Cost: 1 | At 2+ links: go again | At 3+ links: all attacks Draconic this chain |
At 4+ links: this gets +2 power
```
 
**Critical rules note:** these three effects are evaluated **once** at trigger. The Draconic-making effect at 3 links does NOT retroactively trigger the 4-link +2 power condition. You need 4 Draconic links **before** playing Enflame to access its full effect. When you hit 4 links before playing it: 5-power attack, all subsequent attacks become Draconic, go again — a complete chain amplifier. At only 2 links: a 3-power go again extender. Its value scales significantly with when you play it.

## Finishers: Closing the Chain
 
Finishers are played at chain link 4+ where the cost reduction and Rupture effects fire. They deal the most damage per card and do not need go again because the chain ends with them.
 
### Compounding Anger (×2) — Never Cut
 
```
Cost: 3 (reduces by 1 per Draconic chain link) | Power: 5 | Defense: 3
```
 
At 3 Draconic links: **free, 5 power, 3 defense.** The best defensive value of any red card in the deck (blocks 3) while simultaneously being your cheapest high-power attack when the chain is built. The 3 block value is why this card should never be cut — even drawn on a defensive turn, it contributes 3 EV as a block card rather than sitting dead.
 
### Lava Burst (×2) — Rupture Finisher
 
```
Cost: 0 | Power: 3 | Rupture (link 4+): Deal 2 arcane damage
```
 
The premier Rupture finisher. At chain link 4: 3 physical + 2 arcane = **5 EV on a 0-cost card**. The arcane component bypasses all block cards — even if your opponent fully blocks the physical portion, the 2 arcane still connects. Against Iyslander this stacks with Arcane Polarity for maximum pressure.
 
### Salt the Wound (×2) — Scaling Rupture
 
```
Cost: 0 | Power: * | Rupture: Gets +X power where X is total damage dealt this combat chain
```
 
Salt the Wound is your high-variance high-ceiling finisher. In a chain where Rake Over the Coals and Art of the Dragon have pushed every prior attack to 4-5 power, Salt the Wound at Rupture regularly hits **6-8 power**. It also pitches for **2 resources** as a yellow card — making it the most flexible card in the deck (either a scaling finisher or a resource source depending on the turn).
 
### Snatch (×2) — On-Hit Draw
 
```
Cost: 0 | Power: 3 | On hit: Draw a card
```
 
The **only on-hit draw card in the deck.** At chain link 4+ after 3 prior attacks, your opponent typically has no blocks remaining — making Snatch's on-hit draw almost guaranteed. Drawing a card generates 3 EV (Hamilton's card value principle), pushing Snatch to **6 EV when it connects**. Against Lyath Goldmane who draws 5 cards per turn, Snatch is critical for partially closing the hand-size gap. Never cut.

## The Pitch Curve
 
Understanding the pitch distribution is essential for Silver Age, where you play exactly 40 cards drawn from a 48-card pool.
 
### Current Distribution (Revised Deck)
 
| Pitch value | Cards | Qty | % of 40 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blue (3) | Cinderskin Devotion, Dragon Power, Lava Vein Loyalty | ×6 | 15% |
| Yellow (2) | Brand with Cinderclaw, Salt the Wound | ×4 | 10% |
| Red (1) | Everything else | ×30 | 75% |
 
### The Blue Floor Rule
 
With 6 blues in a 40-card deck, the probability of drawing at least 1 blue in a 4-card hand is approximately **46%**. This means **54% of your turns will be all-red hands**. The entire deck is built around this reality — the 0-cost starters exist precisely so an all-red hand can still generate a functional chain.
 
> **The floor:** Never cut blues as sideboard cuts. 6 blues is already the minimum viable floor. Dropping to 5 blues (40% blue hand rate) makes it impossible to reliably fund your 1-cost cards (Flamecall Awakening, Art of the Dragon, Mounting Anger) in all-red hands without sacrificing attacking cards as pure pitch.
 
### All-Red Hand Scenarios
 
**Scenario 1: Ronin Renegade + Flamecall Awakening + Lava Burst**
- Pitch Ronin (1 resource) → funds Flamecall (1 cost) ✅
- But Searing Emberblade at 1 link still costs 2 — need 1 more resource
- Either skip Emberblade or pitch a second card → tight but functional
 
**Scenario 2: Rising Resentment on-hit → Flamecall (free from cost reduction)**
- Rising Resentment on-hit gives cost reduction → Flamecall costs 0
- No pitch needed → perfect all-red turn with 2 cards ✅
 
**Scenario 3: 1 Blue + Flamecall + Art of Dragon + Lava Burst**
- Blue pitches 3 resources → funds Flamecall (1) + Art of Dragon (1) → 1 leftover
- Searing Emberblade at 3 links costs 0 → full chain at no additional cost ✅
- **This is the ideal turn.** 1 blue funds both 1-cost cards with surplus.

## The Sideboard — 8 Cuts Every Game
 
In Silver Age, you sideboard by **removing 8 cards** from your 48-card pool before each game, leaving exactly 40 to play. Equipment choices (head and chest) are separate decisions made from your inventory.
 
### Equipment Decisions
 
| Slot | vs Arcane (Iyslander, Kano) | vs Everyone Else |
|---|---|---|
| Head | Mask of the Swarming Claw (Arcane Barrier) | Blade Beckoner Helm |
| Chest | Smoldering Scales | Blood Scent |
 
### Universal Rules
 
**Cards that never come out** regardless of matchup:
- Rake Over the Coals ×2
- Rise from the Ashes ×2
- Compounding Anger ×2
- Art of the Dragon: Fire ×2
- Snatch ×2
- Fire that Burns Within ×2
- Phoenix Flame ×2
- Flamecall Awakening ×2
 
**Cards that always come out** vs non-arcane heroes:
- Arcane Polarity ×2 — dead card vs any hero dealing zero arcane damage
 
**The blue rule:** All 8 cuts must come from reds and yellows only. Blues are locked.
 
### Matchup-Specific Cuts
 
#### vs Iyslander (Favourable)
*Your game plan: race her life total before Frostbite disrupts your chain*
 
Keep Arcane Polarity ×2 in — it prevents arcane damage that bypasses your blocks entirely.
 
| Cut | Why |
|---|---|
| Wax On ×2 | Her attacks are small — no need for emergency cheap blocks |
| Ronin Renegade ×1 | Weakest starter — cut in offensive matchups |
| Rising Resentment ×1 | On-hit less reliable when she blocks efficiently |
| Enflame the Firebrand ×1 | One copy out in a fast matchup |
| Brand with Cinderclaw (yellow) ×1 | Lowest-value yellow once blues cover resources |
| Salt the Wound ×1 | Keep 1 for Rupture scaling |
 
#### vs Dromai (Roughly Even)
*Your game plan: race her dragon board before it becomes unmanageable. Keep Wax On*
 
| Cut | Why |
|---|---|
| Arcane Polarity ×2 | Zero arcane from Dromai |
| Ronin Renegade ×1 | Vanilla starter cut |
| Rising Resentment ×1 | On-hit less reliable vs board hero |
| Enflame the Firebrand ×1 | One copy out |
| Brand with Cinderclaw (yellow) ×1 | Low-value yellow |
| Salt the Wound ×1 | Keep 1 for Rupture |
| Lava Burst ×1 | Arcane less relevant vs Dromai |
 
*Keep Wax On ×2 in — Dromai's dragons swing for 5–7 early and you need emergency 3-blocks.*
 
#### vs Verdance (Unfavourable)
*Your game plan: every Draconic attack must be at 4+ power. Rake Over the Coals is your most important card.*
 
| Cut | Why |
|---|---|
| Arcane Polarity ×2 | Zero arcane from Verdance |
| Wax On ×2 | Her attacks are manageable |
| Ronin Renegade ×1 | Weakest starter vs high-block hero |
| Rising Resentment ×1 | Hard to connect vs efficient blocker |
| Brand with Cinderclaw (yellow) ×1 | Low-value yellow |
| Enflame the Firebrand ×1 | One copy out |
 
*Without Rake Over the Coals, all your 3-power starters are neutralised for free by a single blue card. With it, every attack crosses the single-block threshold.*
 
#### vs Kayo (Roughly Even)
*Your game plan: block his chain link 4 Rupture turns, race his life total with pumped chains*
 
| Cut | Why |
|---|---|
| Arcane Polarity ×2 | Zero arcane from Kayo |
| Ronin Renegade ×1 | Vanilla starter cut |
| Rising Resentment ×1 | On-hit less reliable under his pressure |
| Enflame the Firebrand ×1 | One copy out |
| Brand with Cinderclaw (yellow) ×1 | Low-value yellow |
| Salt the Wound ×1 | Keep 1 for Rupture |
| Mounting Anger ×1 | Keep 1 for on-hit extension |
 
*Keep Wax On ×2 in — Kayo's Rupture turns hit for 10–15.*
 
#### vs Lyath Goldmane (Favourable — but draws 5 cards)
*Special consideration: Lyath draws 5 cards per turn vs your 4. He has permanent 1-card hand advantage.*
 
The 5-card hand changes your priorities:
- **Snatch becomes critical** — drawing a card on hit partially closes the hand gap
- **Compounding Anger stays in** — free 5-power at link 3 forces him to use 2 of his 5 cards to fully block, leaving only 3 for offence
- **Wax On comes out** — his damage is consistent rather than spiking
 
| Cut | Why |
|---|---|
| Arcane Polarity ×2 | Zero arcane from Lyath |
| Wax On ×2 | No dangerous damage spikes |
| Compounding Anger ×1 | Keep 1 (free 5-power), cut second |
| Ronin Renegade ×1 | Weakest starter vs 5-card blocker |
| Rising Resentment ×1 | On-hit unreliable when he blocks 2 per turn |
| Enflame the Firebrand ×1 | One copy out in fast matchup |

## Game Data: Does the Math Hold?
 
Across 5 logged games in Silver Age, here's how the EV numbers tracked:
 
| Game | Opponent | Avg EV/turn | Avg EV/card | Conversion | Result | Rank |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| G1 | Iyslander | 9.4 | 3.1 | 28.8% | Win | Silver |
| G2 | Dromai | 12.4 | 3.3 | 36.7% | Loss | Silver |
| G3 | Verdance | 12.3 | 3.4 | 20.6% | Win | Gold |
| G4 | Kayo | 11.3 | 3.1 | 48% | Win | Gold |
| G5 | Lyath | 15.0 | 4.13 | 50% | Win | Platinum |
 
The progression tells the story clearly. G1 and G2 averaged below 12.4 EV per turn — below the 14+ constructed target. G5, played with Flamecall Awakening and a second Phoenix Flame added, was the first game to hit both the 14+ EV per turn threshold **and** the 3.5+ EV per card target simultaneously. The deck upgrades are measurably working.
 
The Dromai loss at 12.4 EV (higher than the Iyslander win at 9.4) illustrates that EV alone doesn't tell the whole story — matchup knowledge and sideboarding decisions matter as much as raw numbers. Going into Dromai without Wax On and with Rake Over the Coals absent from the pool were the primary causes of the loss, not execution.

## Key Takeaways
 
**For new Fai players, these are the principles that matter most:**
 
1. **Build to chain link 3 every turn** — this is when the hero ability becomes free, Searing Emberblade becomes free, and Compounding Anger becomes free. Everything in the deck serves this goal.
 
2. **Fire that Burns Within + Phoenix Flame discard is intentional** — you are paying 1 card (Phoenix Flame) for +2 power on an attack. You will always recover the Phoenix Flame. This is a trade you always make.
 
3. **Rake Over the Coals is an instant — timing matters** — play it after seeing your opponent's blocks. If they block your 3-power attacks with 3-block cards, Rake pushes you to 4 power and they need a second card. If they don't block at all, the extra damage is free.
 
4. **Never cut blues when sideboarding** — 6 blues is already the floor. All 8 sideboard cuts come from reds and yellows.
 
5. **The number 4 is your chain link target, the number 7 is your per-card EV target** — every turn decision should be evaluated against these two numbers.
